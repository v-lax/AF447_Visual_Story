Titanic of the Skies

The Crash of Air France Flight 447

On May 31st, 2009 216 passengers and 12 flight crew boarded Air France flight 447 on route to Paris Charles de Gaulle from Rio de Janeiro. None of these individuals would ever make it to their final destination. The crash sent shockwaves throughout the aviation world. Everyone was dumbfounded by the idea that a plane as sophisticated and advanced as the A330, and a crew as experienced as the one onboard AF447, could crash into the Atlantic ocean. The following examines what led AF447 to crash into the Atlantic ocean, killing all 228 individuals on board.
Before we jump into the story and start examining what happened, it is going to be extremely useful to first understand how airplanes work. There are three main concepts that we will cover. These concepts will help you understand the pilots actions and how that impacts the airplane. These concepts are by no means exhaustive. Airplane aerodynamics are extremely complicated but having this basic understanding will help as we walk through this story.
The first concept I want to focus on is lift. Lift (and a combination of a few other items) is what helps a 230 ton piece of metal get up into the sky. It’s also what allows birds to fly. In order to understand lift, we will focus on a key component of any airplane or creature that can fly, the wings. The shape of the wing is what is known as an airfoil. This shape causes the pressure at the bottom of the wing to be higher than the pressure at the top of the wing. This pressure difference creates a lift force. And if our lift force is greater than the gravitational force pulling down on our plane, then our plane is going to climb and increase in altitude.
Now we have got our plane into the sky. How do we keep it in the air and keep the plane moving forward? Our plane stays in the air, constantly moving forward because there is 0 net force acting on the plane. What does that mean? Well, there are 4 main forces acting on an airplane as it is moving through the sky. It experiences a gravitational force (the force pulling the plane toward the ground), a lift force (the force pushing the plane up and away from the ground), a thrust force (the force created by the engines propelling the plane forward), and a drag force (force slowing the plane down as it moves forward and hits air particles). If all these forces are equal and cancel each other out then our plane will stay in the sky and stay in motion. This is basically Newton’s third law of motion, an object in motion stays in motion and an object at rest stays at rest.
Now that we know how a plane stays in the sky, we can look at how a pilot is able to steer it. The cockpit of an Airbus A330, the type of airplane that AF447 was, has a flight control system known as “fly by wire”. If you look in the cockpit you will see that next to both pilot seats there are what look like a video game controller. This is known as the “side-stick” and is what the pilot uses to steer the airplane. In a fly by wire system, inputs to the side stick (this video game-like controller) are converted to electronic signals and transmitted through wires and sent to the flight computers. The flight control computers determine how to move control surfaces on the wings,tail, or elevator. There are three main axes in which an airplane can move. These three main axes are known as pitch, yaw, and roll. Pitch moves the nose of the airplane up and down and is controlled adjusting the flaps on the elevator of the plane. Yaw moves the nose of the airplane left and right and is controlled by adjusting the flaps on the tail of the plane. And roll, moves the airplane along the axis running from the nose to the tail of an airplane, and is controlled by the flaps located on both of the main wings.
01:35:00 - We will pick up our story at 1 hour and 35 minutes. The Captain, Marc Dubois, is pilot not flying (PNF), and is sitting in the left seat in the cockpit. First officer, Pierre Cedric Bonin, is pilot flying (PF), and is sitting in the right seat in the cockpit. Because this is a long haul flight, there was also a second First officer, David Robert, who was outside of the cockpit in the crew quarters, on his break. The airplane arrives at INTOL point. INTOL point is what is known as a waypoint. A waypoint is just a set of coordinates that refers to the plane’s position along a path. Pilots use waypoints to navigate to the final destination and use it to communicate with air traffic control along the flight path.
A few moments later Bonin (PF), modifies scale on his flight display. After adjusting the display, Bonin notes “...a thing straight ahead”. Captain Dubois confirms. Unlike most other flights flying through the area at that time, the pilots of AF447 have not updated their flight path to avoid the worst of the storms. The air outside of the aircraft is also unusually warm (still really cold but 12 degrees warmer than usual) and this prevents the aircraft from climbing to avoid the inclement weather. So instead they charge head on into a group of clouds.
01:51:00 - As they entered this cloud layer the crew dimmed the lighting in the cockpit and turned on the headlights “to see” what was ahead of them. The cockpit also became illuminated by a strange natural phenomena known as “St.Elmos Fire”. It occurs at high altitudes with thunderstorms, and occurs because the surrounding air particles become statically charged.
2:00:00 - At this moment, Captain Dubois leaves the cockpit to get some rest and first officer David Robert, takes his spot as PNF in the left seat of the cockpit. At this stage, the aircraft has just crossed SALPU point and has entered the Intertropical Convergence Zone. This is an area near the equator that is infamous for having severe thunderstorms. Bonin makes a call to one of the flight attendants informing them that they should expect some rough turbulence in a few minutes, and that he would call back once they were out of it. Bonin never makes the call back.
2:04:00 - The airplane is approaching ARARO point. At this stage, the aircraft is flying level at an altitude of 35,000 feet at a speed of Mach .82. The pitch attitude or the angle of the nose of the airplane in relation to the horizon was 2.5 degrees and the angle of attack of the plane was 3 degrees. All normal and nothing out of the ordinary here.
The angle of attack is the angle at which the airplane wings meet the oncoming air. The higher the angle of attack the greater the lift efficiency, BUT (and this is a really really big but) only up to a certain point. If the angle attack exceeds a certain amount, the oncoming air can no longer smoothly flow over the top of the wing, decreasing lift, which causes the aircraft to go into what is known as a stall. This happens with all airplanes and has absolutely nothing to do with the engines. When a stall occurs, the airplane wings are at such a steep angle that the power and thrust provided by the engines of the plane can not exceed the drag forces experienced by the airplane. The aircraft then enters a nose high descent straight into the ground. Now, the only way to exit a stall, is to do something that seems really counter-intuitive. You have to push the nose of the airplane down and dive, so that the oncoming air can begin to smoothly flow over the wing again, thereby producing lift and taking the airplane out of the stall.
2:00:05 - The aircraft is currently operating in what is known as “Normal Law”. Under this mode, the flight computers control a large portion of the “fly by wire” system we mentioned above. If for some reason the pilot does something by mistake and pulls the side stick back, pushing the nose of the airplane up, normal law actually prevents this input from fully being translated to changes on the control surfaces on the plane. The computer basically says, “Im in normal mode, and i just received an input that would be too dangerous for this altitude. Because this action would cause a stall at this altitude, I will restrict this input from the pilot and not allow it to go through.” It’s a great fail safe and has contributed a lot to why air travel is so safe these days. AF447 is currently operating under “Normal Law” and everything seems to be going ok, but not for long.
2:08:00 - Robert notices that they are headed towards an area of intensive activity and asks Bonin if he can move the aircraft left, so that they can dodge this activity. Bonin rolls the plane 20 degrees to the left and also reduces the airspeed from .82 mach to .8 mach. This slightly increased the angle of attack on the airplane. The sound of tiny ice crystals hitting the windshield of the aircraft could clearly be heard in the cockpit.
2:10:05 - Unknown to both pilots, these ice crystals were clogging up the airplane’s pitot tubes. The pitot tubes are located on the front of all major aircraft and are instruments that provide vital airspeed data to the aircraft’s computers. Because the ice crystals clogged up the pitot tubes, accurate air speed data stopped being sent to the flight computers, and without accurate airspeed data, AF447’s autopilot turned off, sounding an alarm in the cockpit, handing control of the aircraft fully over to the pilots. This also switched the mode on the flight computers from the existing “Normal Law” to “Alternate Law”. In alternate law, the pilot’s inputs are not as scrutinized by the flight computer as under “normal law”, which means that the flight computers can’t prevent any errors or prevent stalls from happening. Everything that just occurred was a normal and logical response by the airplane and was designed this way by the airplane engineers. At this moment, Bonin verbally says “I have the controls.”
2:10:07 - Now remember, the airplane is currently flying level (both wings and nose level to the horizon) and the engine thrust set to consistently deliver a nominal .8 mach. The only significant error was the loss of the indication of airspeed, not the actual airspeed itself. All Bonin needs to do is keep the aircraft straight and level and maintain these current conditions. As Bonin takes control, he realizes the plane begins to roll slightly to the right and to correct it all Bonin needs to do is move his side stick a little bit to the left to counteract the roll right. But for some unknown reason, Bonin makes a nose up and left input. Now its not a slight pulling back of the side stick. Bonin jerks the side stick back considerably and to the left. Because Bonin makes this nose up and left input, an alarm (Altitude alert C cord) in the cockpit sounds warning the pilots that the aircraft has left its recommended altitude and has begun climbing because of Bonin’s nose up input. The airplane then sounded the stall warning twice in a row. Now remember, the plane’s computers are in alternate law so it is not going to correct Bonin’s mistake. Neither pilot acknowledged the altitude or stall warning.
2:10:16 - The airplane is now climbing at a blistering rate, almost 7000ft/minute. Both pilots acknowledge that they have lost their airspeed indications. But as the aircraft continues to climb it’s also losing a tremendous amount of speed. During the next ten seconds Bonin brings the pitch of the aircraft from 2.5 degrees to 11 degrees. During these next ten seconds, Rober starts to read out the warning messages in a disorganized manner. He verbally says “Alternate Law Protections”, which means that the airplane has moved from normal to alternate law and that stalls can occur. But each time the stall warning sounds, both pilots seem to not acknowledge it at all. It’s not clear that Robert processed what he was reading from the screen or if Bonin ever heard anything that Robert was saying at this time. Because if they did, they would have acknowledged the stall warnings and instead of pulling up, Bonin could have pushed the nose of the plane down, to recover from the stall.
2:10:27 - Robert tells Bonin, “Pay attention to your speed. Pay attention to your speed.” By this Robert probably means the vertical speed because their air speed is still currently invalid.
2:10:28 - Bonin responds, “Ok, ok, I am descending...”
2:10:31- Robert tells Bonin, “Decend… It says we are going up… It says we are going up.... Descend.”
2:10:36 - The pitot tube on the left side of the aircraft finally becomes unclogged and begins working again. This means that Robert, the pilot sitting in the left seat has accurate air speed indications on his display. Now during this whole time, Robert has actually no idea whether or not Bonin is making the necessary side stick inputs. That is because Robert’s side stick and Bonin’s stick are not linked. This is how most Airbus aircraft are designed. If Bonin moves side stick, Robert’s side stick stays stationary. If both pilots move their side stick at the same time, then a warning sounds off saying “Dual Input”, and flight computers split the difference. If Bonin and Rober were flying a Boeing aircraft, they would both have what is called yolk. Its a long column that sits in front of the pilots seat. Now if one pilot moves his yolk or control column in a boeing, the other pilot’s yolk/control column would move as well because both are linked. Robert would be able to identify exactly what Bonin was doing. In this A330 that was unfortunately not the case.
2:10:38- Bonin eases back on the stick and for slight moment the aircraft begins to pick up some speed as the nose is lowered. A few degrees lower and Bonin can reduce the climb and get the aircraft back on track, but for some reason Bonin does not do this. He push back on the stick again.
2:10:41 - not knowing what is happening, Robert makes a call to Captain Dubois asking him to come to cockpit immediately.
2:10:51 - Bonin continues to make nose up inputs, raising the pitch of the aircraft to 13 degrees above the horizon. Again the stall warning sounds. The aircraft begins to shake violently.
2:10:55 - The stall warning continues sound. The thrust levers are positioned in the TO/GA detent. TO/GA stands for take off/go around. Now the reason the thrust is at this position is because this is the setting used by pilots during take offs and go arounds (when they need to abort a landing), when they need to increase altitude and speed efficiently. Only issues is that this only works at low altitudes near sea level, NOT AT CRUISING altitude. At this altitude where the air is much thinner, engines generate less thrust and the wings produce much less left. About a few seconds later the air speed indication on Bonin’s display became valid again. After this point, it was all pilot error.
2:11:06 - The aircraft reaches its maximum altitude of about 38,000 feet. With the engine at full power and an angle of attack of 16 degrees, the plane begins to decend and fall out of the sky. If Bonin just let go of the side stick, the plane’s pitch would decrease, increase the flow of air over the top of the wings, and pulling the plane out of the stall. But Bonin, doesnt let go and pulls and holds the side stick all the way back. At this time, the plane is shaking so much that it’s extremely difficult to keep the plane's wings level.
2:11:37 - Robert takes control of the plane for a few seconds, without letting Bonin know that he was taking control. Almost immediately after Robert takes control of the plane, Bonin takes control back.
2:11:42 - Captain Dubois finally comes in the cockpit to see what is happening. Captain Dubois asks, “What the hell are you doing?”. And Bonin responds that they have lost control of the airplane.
2:11:47 - The aircraft falls back down to it’s original altitude of 35,000 feet but continues to descend at a rate of almost 10,000ft/min. At this moment the stall warning stopped sounding because the angle of attack was so high that the flight computers rejected the data as invalid. The recorded angle of attack was an incredible 41 degrees. The right wing was down by about 30 degrees causing the plane to arc way off its set flight path. The aircraft would maintain these characteristics all the way till it hit the Atlantic ocean.
2:12:02 - Now because the stall warning had stopped sounding, the pilots may have assumed that all was ok again (when in reality things were not ok). Each time bonin actually did lower the nose of the plane, the computer began to receive accurate data again and the stall warning began to sound. Thinking that he was in stall again Bonin then pulled back on his side stick, increasing the pitch of the plane, and increasing the angle of attack, such that the computer rejected the data being provided, and the stall warning stopped. This was a nightmarish negative feedback loop. The plane was now loosing altitude at a rate of 15,000ft/min/
2:12:14 - Robert asks Captain Dubois, “What do you think? What do you think? What should we do?”
2:12:15- Captain Dubois says, “I don’t know.” Captain Dubois urges Bonin to level the wings but that does extremely little to solve their actual problem.
2:13:32 - Robert quickly takes back control, pitches the nose of the airplane down, but as he does that Bonin pushes the nose of the plane up. The Dual Input warning sounds again and the plane maintains a upward pitch. Bonin says, “We are reaching flight level 100.” , which means their current altitude is 10,000ft. This was the last moment in which a recovery would have been possible. There are only a handful of pilots who would have been able to execute the maneuver necessary to save this plane.
2:13:40 - Robert tells Bonin, “Climb, Climb, Climb…”. Bonin responds, “But I have had my stick back this whole time!”. To this Captain Dubois says, “No, do not climb!”. What Captain Dubois meant to say was “Do not pitch up!” because it was because their high pitch that they were falling out of the sky. They actually needed to “climb”.
2:14:17 - The ground proximity warning system began to sound because it detected the ocean was fast approaching. Warning of “Sink Rate” and “Pull Up” began to sound.
2:14:28 - The recording stops.
